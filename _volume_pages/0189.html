{% raw %}* Rathaus, or Town Hall (PL R, 26), an imposing brick edifice{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with tasteful terracotta embellishments and granite facings, erected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 1861-69 from the plans of Waesemaun. on the site of the old{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rathaus to which the Gerichtslaube (p. 198) belonged. The structure{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is 325 ft. long, 288 ft. deep, and 88 ft. high to the top of the attic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stage above the third story, while the tall tower rises to a height of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}243 ft. (to the top of the flag-staff 318 ft.). The dial-plates of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}clock measure 15'/a ft. in diameter. Like many of the other modern{% endraw %}
{% raw %}buildings of Berlin, the Rathaus exhibits a union of a mediseval{% endraw %}
{% raw %}structural system (round-arched) with Renaissance details, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}resembles the edifices of North Italy. The open-work projections{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at the angles of the tower recall the Cathedral of Laon. — The bronze{% endraw %}
{% raw %}statues of Emp. William I. and Elector Frederick I., in the niches{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by the portal, were executed by Keil and En.cke; the reliefs of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}balconies represent scenes from the life in Old and New Berlin.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The architectural decoration of the principal rooms on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}First Floor (adm., see p. 39), which gave a new impetus to in¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dustrial art in Berlin, is due to Kolscher.{% endraw %}
