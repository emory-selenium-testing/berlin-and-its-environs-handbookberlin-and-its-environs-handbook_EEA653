{% raw %}BERLIN. History of Art.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Contemporaneously with the rise of architecture the Applied{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Arts, which since the misfortunes of Napoleon's time had made no pro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gress, received a stimulating impetus towards the picturesque style{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Renaissance. The increasing prosperity of the town after{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1870 caused a demand for more luxurious and more artistic domestic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}interiors. The Museum of Industrial Art (p. 132), founded in 1868,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}made its influence strongly felt in this field. The increased activity{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in this branch of art is shown not only by the extraordinary multipli¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cation of artistic upholsterers and the like, but also by the long list{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of excellent artists who devote themselves in whole or part to decor¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ative art. Among these may be mentioned, besides architects, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}painters Doepler, Koch, Lechter, and the sculptors 0. Lessing,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Wiedman, and Vogel. Chandeliers and artistic designs in brackets{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for gas and electric lighting are nowhere better made than in Berlin;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}while cabinet-making and carpet-weaving hold a high place among{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the industries of the city. The arts of the jeweller and the gold¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}smith, enamelling, and the manufacture of brass and bronze articles,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are also carried on very successfully. The Royal Porcelain Factory{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is meutioned at p. 181.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}As regards the General Aspect, Berlin suffers from the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dead level of its site, and also, since three-quarters of its buildings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are quite modern, from a certain lack of historical interest. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Church of St. Nicholas (p. 157), the Church of St. Mary (p. 153), the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Kloster-Kirche (p. 156), and the Chapel of the Holy Ghost (p. 154){% endraw %}
{% raw %}are practically the only buildings remaining of the old town (p. 46),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which consisted of some narrow, crooked streets of dwelling-houses{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(pp. 147, 152), and a few larger cloisters and hospitals grouped{% endraw %}
{% raw %}round the two Town Halls. With improved means of locomotion the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}inner town has now gradually become the commercial nucleus of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Berlin, like the City in London. Immense and palatial buildings{% endraw %}
{% raw %}have arisen, occupied from floor to ceiling by business offices and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}warerooms alone. The approaches to the old town have been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}widened, new ones have been built, and the Spree has been cleared{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of obstructions. The neighbourhood of the Royal Palace (p. 65){% endraw %}
{% raw %}has been remodelled in harmony with the baroque forms of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}palace itself. The Lustgarten, the Opern-Platz, and the Linden{% endraw %}
{% raw %}together form a broad and magnificent thoroughfare of the first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}rank, such as may possibly be paralleled in Vienna, but certainly not{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in either London or Paris. The street known as Unter den Linden{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 55), which had hardly lived up to its. ancient reputation, has again{% endraw %}
{% raw %}become one of the chief arteries of traffic. The old houses are dis¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appearing, magnificent hotels and business premises have sprung{% endraw %}
{% raw %}up, while the avenues of trees and the footpaths have been altered{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and modernized. The system on which the Friedrichstadt, to the{% endraw %}
