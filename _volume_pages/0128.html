{% raw %}This museum, situated to the N.W. of the last-mentioned, is not{% endraw %}
{% raw %}directly accessible thence, but must be reached by a circuit via the Iron{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Bridge and the Kupfergraben (8 min. from the Old Museum; comp. Plan,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. 55).— Tramways: through the Charlotten-Str. (W.) see p. 145, through{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Museum-Str. and from the Kupfergraben see p. 70.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}The **Emperor Frederick Museum (PI. R, 23), situated at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the N.W. end of the 'museum island', beyond the Stadtbahn, was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}built by Ihne in 1898-1903 and opened in 1904. Two bridges, from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Monbijou-Strasse (p. 145) and from the street Am Kupfergraben,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lead to the entrance, at the N.W. angle, opposite which rises the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}equestrian Statue of Emperor Frederick III, by Maison. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Italian baroque style has been adopted for the building, the two{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chief fagades of which rise directly from the Spree and the Kupfer¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}graben , while the third is turned towards the Stadtbahn. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}corner with the entrance is rounded off into a semicircle; six mas¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sive columns support its upper story, which is crowned by the main{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dome. A smaller dome, above the rear staircase, rises over the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}centre of the facade towards the Stadtbahn. On the attic are ten{% endraw %}
{% raw %}statues by A. Vogel and Widemann, six of which represent the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Arts and four the most prominent Art Cities.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The museum contains the sculptures of the Christian epoch, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}picture-gallery, and the cabinet of coins, hitherto exhibited in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Old and New Museums. To these have been added collections of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}early-Christian, Byzantine, Coptic, and Persian-Mohammedan art.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The collections are not strictly divided from each other. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}27 rooms and cabinets on the groundfloor contain, on the N. side,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the early-Christian, Byzantine, Coptic, Romanesque, and Gothic{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Monuments, on the S. side the German and Italian Sculptures, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Cabinet of Coins. But among the German sculptures a certain{% endraw %}
{% raw %}number of easel-pictures are exhibited, while all the bronze and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}most of the marble Italian sculptures are to be found on the first{% endraw %}
{% raw %}floor. In the chief room on the groundfloor, moreover, are collected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the largest altar-pieces, both plastic and painted. The 45 rooms{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and cabinets on the first floor are occupied by the picture-gallery:{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on the N. side the Latin Schools (Italian, French, Spanish), on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}S. side the Germanic Schools (Dutch, German, Flemish). Along with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the former are exhibited the Italian bronzes, marble sculptures, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}glazed terracotta works (Delia Robbia), while with the others are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}shown the collections of James Simon (presented in 1904), Adolph{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Thiem (acquired in 1904), Wesendonk, and Carstanjen (both on loan).{% endraw %}
