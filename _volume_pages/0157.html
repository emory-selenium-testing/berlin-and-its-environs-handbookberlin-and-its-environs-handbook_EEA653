{% raw %}No. 73, on the right, is the house of the Minister of the House¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}hold, erected in 1734-37 for Count Schwerin. No. 74 is the Reichs-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}amt des lunern, or Imperial Home Office, where the German{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Federal Council meets (comp. p. 139). No. 65, opposite, to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}left, is the office of the Minister of Justice; No. 64, the Privy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Office for Civil Affairs, erected by Vohl; and No. 63, the Stute{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ministry, the Offices of the State Lottery, and the General-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Ordeus-Commission. Then on the right, Nos. 75-76, the Foreign-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Office. No. 77 is the Imperial Chancellery and the Residence{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Chancellor, originally built about 1738, rebuilt in 1875-76,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and occupied by Prince Bismarck till March, 1X90. (The Congress{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of European Powers for the settlement of the Eastern Question in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1878 took place in the large hall in front.) No. 7x is the Palace{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Prince Pless, designed by the French architect Destailleur in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1872-75 in the style of the period of Louis XIII.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Wilhelm-Platz (PI. R, 19, 22) is adorned with flower-beds{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and with Statues of six heroes of the three Silesian wars of Fred¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erick the Great: Schwerin, who fell, grasping the colours, at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Prague in 1757; Winterfeldt, Frederick's favourite, who fell at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Moys, near Gorlitz, in 1757, both by Kiss; Seydlitz, the hero of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rossbach, who died in 1773; Keith, who fell at Hochkirch in 1758,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}both by Tassaert (d. 1788); the gallant Zieten, who died in 1786;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Pi-ince Leopold of Auhalt-Dessau, the victor at Kesselsdorf,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who died in 1747, the last two by G. Schadow.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The former marble statues were replaced in 18H2 by bronze statues{% endraw %}
{% raw %}copied from the original figures, with the exception of those of Schwerin{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Winterfeldt, who had been inappropriately represented in Roman{% endraw %}
{% raw %}costume, and are now in the Emperor Frederick Museum (p. 120).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}On the N. side of the Wilhelm-Platz, Nos. 8-9, is the Palace{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Prince Frederick Leopold, erected in 1737 and remodelled by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Schinkel in 1827-28. — On the E. side lie the Kur & Neumarkische{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Haupt-Ritterschafts-Direktion (No. 6) and the Kuiserhof (p. 3),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}built in 1873-75 by Von der Hude & Hennicke. Behind the latter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the Church of the Trinity (Dreifaltigkeits-Kirche), erected{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 1737-39 and enlarged in 1885-86; opposite the door is a bust, by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Schaper, of Schleiermacher (p. 171), the eminent preacher and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}theologian, who was pastor here from 1809 until his death in 1834.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— On the S. side of the Wilhelm-Platz (No. 1) is the Imperial{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Treasury (Reichs-Schatzamt).{% endraw %}
