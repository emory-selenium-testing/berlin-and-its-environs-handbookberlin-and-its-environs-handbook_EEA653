{% raw %}Unter den Linden. BERLIN. Section 1. 57{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the E. end of Unter den Linden rises the '^Monument of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Frederick the Great (PI. R, 23), 44 ft. in height, an impressive{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and masterly work by Ranch, erected in 1851. On the top the great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}king is represented on horseback, with an ermine mantle and his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}crutch-handled stick. The pedestal is richly decorated with reliefs.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The uppermost section contains scenes from Frederick's life and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}figures of Moderation, Justice, Wisdom, and Strength at the corners.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At the corners of the central section are four equestrian figures:{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Prince Henry of Prussia and Duke Ferdinand of Brunswick on the E.,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Generals Zieten and Seydlitz on the W. Between these are placed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}spirited lifesize groups of other contemporaries and officers of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}king, including Prince August Wilhelm and Keith (E. side), Kleist,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Winterfeldt, and Tauentzien (N. side), Leopold of Anhalt-Dessau{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Schwerin (S. side), Lessing, Kant, and Graun (W. side). The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lowest section contains the dedication and the names of other dis¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tinguished men, chiefly soldiers of the time of Frederick.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the right (S.) of the monument is the Palace of Emperor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}"William I. (PI. R, 23; adm., see p. 38), at present" belonging to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Prince Heinrich, erected by K. F. Langhans (p. 50) in 1834-36;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the decorations of the interior are by Strack. Entrance on the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}right of the palace, between it and the adjacent 'Niederlandische{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Palais' (where the cards of admission are obtained). Description{% endraw %}
{% raw %}with views 50 pf.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Ground Floor contains the simple rooms of the Emperor,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}crowded with furniture and souvenirs of all kinds. The Fahnenzimmer,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the E. of the main entrance, contains a picture by Camphausen (William I.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}entering Berlin in 1871). Beyond the Ministers' Room is the Emperor's{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Study, from the corner-window of which he was wont to watch the pass¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing guard. It is preserved unchanged and contains many reminiscences{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the old Kaiser. Behind the library is the bedroom (not shown) in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which he died on March 9th, 1888. — The Staircase is adorned with three{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Victories by Ranch and other sculptures. — On the Upper Floor are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Apartments of the Empress Augusta, including her Study, contain¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing a picture by Pesne of Frederick the Great as a child, and the room{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(not shown) in which she died on Jan. 7th, 1890. — The W. part of this floor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is occupied by the handsome Reception Rooms.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Opposite the palace to the N. is the 'Academy Quarter' (PI. R, 23),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}where new buildings in the baroque style after Ihne's designs are{% endraw %}
{% raw %}being erected for the Academy of Science (founded after Leibnitz's{% endraw %}
{% raw %}plan in 1700, see p. 47), the Royal Library (p. 58), and the University{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Library (p. 58).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the N. of the Academy Quarter, Dorotheen-Str. 7-8, is the Berlin{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Chamber of Commerce, a baroque building by Cremer & Wolffenstein.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Adjacent, Nos. 9-10, is the University Library (196,000 vols.); on No. 10{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are busts of A. S. Margraf (d. 1782), the discoverer of beet-sugar, and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of F. K Achard (d. 1821), the founder of the beet-sugar industry.{% endraw %}
