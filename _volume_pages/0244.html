{% raw %}To the E. of Potsdam we ferry from the Holzmarkt-Str., out¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}side the Berliner Tor, to Babelsberg (see below). The Neue Konigs-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Strasse, which begins at the Berliner Tor, passes the Barracks of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Hussar Guards (right) and of the Gardes du Corps (left) to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Glienicke Bridge ('Kaiser-Friedrich-Brilcke', rebuilt in 1907;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tramway, see pp. 189,199). The bronze groups in front of the latter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}barracks (soldiers of the regiment leading horses) are by Kiss.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At Klein-Glienicke (see p. 187), beyond the bridge to the left,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is the Puluce of Prince Frederick Leopold, built for Prince{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Charles (d. 1833), the prince's grandfather, by Schinkel. The park{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is not accessible; at the corner near the Glienicke Bridge is a ro¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tunda, built by Schinkel in imitation of the Monument of Lysi-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}crates at Athens.{% endraw %}

{% raw %}ing Chdteuu of Glienicke, originally built in 1678 by the Great{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Elector, restored and enlarged in 1856 by Prince Frederick Charles{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(d. 1885) and subsequently by his son, Prince Frederick Leopold{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(no admittance). — The bay of the Havel, on which the chateau lies,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}is connected by the Teltow Canal with the Griebnitz-See, to the E.;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}There are stations of the steamers to the Neu-Babelsberg station{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(p. 187) in the village near the Biirgershof Restaurant and near the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}canal bridge.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the S. of the canal bridge is the principal entrance to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}park and the palace of Babelsberg, a visit to which occupies iy2 hr.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Numerous stones with way-marks.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The picturesque *Chateau of Babelsberg was erected in the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}English Gothic style by Schinkel in 1835, and extended in 1843-49{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Strack. It stands in a beautiful park, laid out by Prince{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Piickler. The interior of the chateau is simply but tastefully decor¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ated in the unassuming style which prevailed in Germany about{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the middle of last century. Emp. William I. invariably spent the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}latter half of summer here, and his study and bedroom as well as{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the reception rooms are shown to visitors. The walls are hung with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}numerous works of art, mainly of the early Berlin and Dusseldorf{% endraw %}
{% raw %}schools. Memorials of the campaigns of 1849,1864,1866, and 1870-71{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are also preserved here. The N. windows command charming views.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}— In front of the palace is the Gerhard Fountain, a present from{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the architects of Cologne Cathedral; and behind the palace is a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}monument with the Archangel Michael, by Kiss.{% endraw %}
