{% raw %}1. Unter den linden. Platz am Opernhaus.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The handsomest and busiest part of Berlin, which likewise com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}prises the most interesting historical associations, is the line of streets{% endraw %}
{% raw %}extending from the Brandenburg Gate to the Royal Palace, consist¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ing of *Unter den Linden (PI. R, 20, 23), the Platz am Opern¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}haus, and the Platz am Zeughaus. The Linden, a street 198 ft. in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}width, deriving its name from the lime-trees (interspersed with{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chestnuts) with which it is planted, resembles the Boulevards of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Paris, although inferior in length, and is flanked with handsome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}palaces, spacious hotels and restaurants, and attractive shops, which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}since the end of the 19th. cent, have gradually replaced the older{% endraw %}
{% raw %}buildings (see p. 52). The Linden is to Berlin what Bond Street{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and Piccadilly are to London, the corner of the Friedrich-Strasse{% endraw %}
{% raw %}presenting a most animated picture, especially in the afternoon{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and evening. The length of the street from the Brandenburg Gate{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the Monument of Frederick the Great is about 2/3 M., to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}palace-gate about 1 M.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The *Brandenburg Gate (PI. R, 20,19), at the W. end of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Linden, forms the entrance to the town from the Tiergarten (see{% endraw %}
{% raw %}p. 136). It was erected in 1789-93 by K. G. Langhans in imitation{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Propylsea at Athens, and has five different passages, separated{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by massive Doric columns. The material is sandstone. The structure{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(85 ft. in height, including the figure, and 205 ft. in width) is sur¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mounted by a Quadriga of Victory, in copper, by G. Schadow{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1794). This was taken to Paris by the French in 1807, but restored{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in 1814. The iron cross (under the eagle) was added after its return.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The gate is flanked with open Colonnades for foot-passengers, built{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Strack in 1868; the statue of Mars in that to the S. is by Schadow.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tramways (pp. 14-22) run from the Brandenburg Gate and the Reichstag{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Building (No. 13 from the latter only) to the Anhalt Station (Nos. 1, 7,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}14,15), Charlottenburg N., Donhoff-Platz (6, 9, 13), Friedrich-Strasse Station{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(13, N, 0, S, T), Gesundbrunnen (23), Gorlitz Station (13), Hackescher{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Markt (52, 56), Halle Gate (1, 7, 14, 15), Lehrte Station and Moabit (6,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}7, 9, 13, 14, 16, 23, 24), Museum Island (N, O, S. T), Neues Tor (51, 57),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Nollendorf-Platz (51, 52, 56, 57), Opern-Platz (13), Potsdam Gate (1, 6, 7,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}9, 14, 15, 23, 24, 51, 52, 56, 57), Rixdorf (7, 15), Silesian Station (1, 6, 9),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Schoneberg (23, 24, 51, 56, 57), Spittelmarkt (6, 9), Stettin Station (51, 57),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Zoological Gardens (0).{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Between the gate and the beginning of the Linden lies the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Pariser-Platz , so named after the victories of 1814, and embell¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ished with pleasure-grounds. No. 4, on the S. side, is the Royal Aca¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}demy of Art, in the former palace of Count Arnim. The Academy{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Art was established in 1694 under Frederick I. by Sehliiter and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Terwesten (exhibitions, see p. 37). No. 5, on the N. side, is the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}French Embassy.{% endraw %}
