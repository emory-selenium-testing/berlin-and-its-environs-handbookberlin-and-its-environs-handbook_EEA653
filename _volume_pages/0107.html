{% raw %}First Floor.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The first floor of the New Museum and the passage leading to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Old Museum are occupied by the very extensive and valuable Col¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lection of Casts from the antique. Scientific catalogue by Friede-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}richs (2nd edit, by Wolters, 1885; 12 Jl). Room III contains also{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Greek landscapes, and Room X mural paintings from the Greek{% endraw %}
{% raw %}heroic myths. — Rooms XI and XII contain the casts of the sculp¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tures discovered during the excavations carried on by Ernst Curtius{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(d. 1896) in 1876-81 at Olympia at the expense of the German{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Empire, and the duplicates of original sculptures found on the same{% endraw %}
{% raw %}occasion.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The originals of the first-named sculptures remain at Olympia. The{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chief objects here are the two Pediment Groups from the Temple of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Zeus, by an unknown sculptor of the beginning of the 5th cent. B.C.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The E. Pediment represents the preparations for the chariot race between{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Polops and Oenomaos, which Curtius describes as follows. In the centre{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stands Zeus, with Pelops and Hippodamia on his right, and King Oeno¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}maos and his wife Sterope on his left; to the right and left of these{% endraw %}
{% raw %}appear the two four-horse chariots, held by kneeling charioteers, beyond{% endraw %}
{% raw %}which, on the left, are a seated man, a kneeling girl, and finally the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}recumbent figure of the river-god Alpheus, and, on the right, a bald-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}headed old man, a boy seated on the ground, and the river-god Cla-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}deus. On the W. Pediment the struggle between the Lapithse and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Centaurs at the marriage of Pirithous is depicted. In the middle is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Apollo extending his right hand in a commanding gesture, while on each{% endraw %}
{% raw %}side are groups of combatants; to the left a Centaur abducting a woman{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and attacked by Pirithous; to the right, Theseus protecting a woman{% endraw %}
{% raw %}against her abductors; to the left again, a Centaur carrying off a boy,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and to the right, a kneeling Lapith strangling a Centaur; the succeeding{% endraw %}
{% raw %}groups on each side resemble the first groups, but with the figures in a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}kneeling position; the composition finally terminates with a recumbout{% endraw %}
{% raw %}female form at each end.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The chief single figures are those of the Hermes of Praxiteles, the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}only authenticated and at the same time well preserved masterpiece of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the greatest Greek sculptor of the 4th cent., and of the Nike of Paeonins,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erected at the beginning of the Peloponnesian War, in which the sculptor{% endraw %}
{% raw %}has most admirably succeeded in representing the goddess of Victory in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the act of flying.{% endraw %}
