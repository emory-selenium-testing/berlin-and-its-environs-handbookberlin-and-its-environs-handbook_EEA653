{% raw %}BERLIN.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}History.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Frederick the Great (1740-86) was unwearied in his efforts{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to extend and embellish his capital, though he seldom made it his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}residence. In G. W. von Knobelsdorff (1699-1753) he found an ar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chitect who was eminently capable of executing his plans. Thus in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}1743 he erected the Opera House (p. 58) in a noble, almost class¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ical style, which presented a marked contrast to the capricious and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}degraded taste of the age. As the great monarch, however, had a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}strong predilection for designing his new buildings in person and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for materially altering the designs submitted to him, he found the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}less independent successors of Knobelsdorff more subservient to his{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wishes. The Palace of Prince Henry (now the University; p. 58),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Church of St.Hedwig (p. 59), the Royal Colonnades (p. 152),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Library (p. 58), and the Domed Towers in the Gendarmen-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Markt (p. 121) are the principal edifices of this period. Frederick also{% endraw %}
{% raw %}presented his citizens and officials with several hundred building-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sites, but characteristically insisted that, however modest the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}houses erected on them might be, they should present palatial{% endraw %}
{% raw %}facades towards the street. Commerce and industry (banking, mar¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}itime commerce, the manufacture of china, silk-culture, weaving){% endraw %}
{% raw %}were fostered; the Academy of Sciences, under the auspices of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}French savants, awoke to new life; and the collections of art were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}materially increased. At the same time a new intellectual era{% endraw %}
{% raw %}began to dawn, and to this period belong the authors Lessing{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1729-81), Moses Mendelssohn (1729-86), and Nicolai (1733-1811),{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and the painter and engraver Chodowiecki (1726-1801). Although{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Berlin suffered severely during the Seven Tears' War, having been{% endraw %}
{% raw %}twice occupied by foreign troops (1757 and 1760), the population{% endraw %}
{% raw %}had increased by the end of Frederick's reign to 145,000.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Under Frederick William II. (1786-97), Frederick's successor,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the population increased much more rapidly, and in the year 1800{% endraw %}
{% raw %}it amounted to no fewer than 172,000. Considerable progress{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was also made in the province of art. In 1793 K. G. Langhans{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1733-1808), following the example of Knobelsdorff, erected the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Brandenburg Gate (p. 55) in the classical style, while G. Scha-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dow's Quadriga, with which it was adorned, achieved a new{% endraw %}
{% raw %}triumph in the province of sculpture. The architects Gentz and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Gilly also adopted the classical style, while J. A. Carstens, a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}native of Schleswig, who began his career in 1788 as professor at{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the Berlin Academy, inaugurated the revival of classical taste in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}painting. The theatre, formerly devoted to French plays, was now{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dedicated to the national German drama, which was zealously{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cultivated from the year 1796 onwards under the auspices of Iffland{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(d. 1814).{% endraw %}
