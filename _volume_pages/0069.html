{% raw %}art, science, and commerce began to flourish anew. The uni¬{% endraw %}

{% raw %}distinguished architect was Friedrich Schinkel (1781-1841), who{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was equally capable as a painter, as his sketches for the frescoes{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the hall of the old museum testify (see p. 72), and many ad¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mirable buildings, both in the classical and Gothic style, were{% endraw %}
{% raw %}erected by this great master in Berlin and the environs. It was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}his aim to build as the ancient Greeks would have built had they{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lived among us, and it was due to his teaching that the foundation-{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stone of a national artistic revival was laid. He was the architect{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the Royal Guard House (1818; p. 59), the Royal Theatre (1821;{% endraw %}

{% raw %}Glienicke (p. 198), and Charlottenhof (p. 196), and the Church of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}St. Nicholas at Potsdam (p. 192), while his numerous designs exer¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cised no inconsiderable influence on the architecture of other coun¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}tries. Berlin architects long counted it the highest fame to be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}reckoned among his pupils. As Schinkel reigned supreme at Berlin{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the province of architecture, so did Chr. D. Rauch (1777-1857){% endraw %}
{% raw %}in that of sculpture, eclipsing his senior, G. Schadow, and still more{% endraw %}
{% raw %}so his contemporary, Fr. Tieck. In him the hero-worship of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}period of the wars of independence found an admirable illustrator,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and portrait-sculpture now received a new impulse. Far inferior to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}these architects and sculptors were the painters of this period (Wach,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}K. Begas, Hensel, Klober, K. Blechen, and F. Krilger), whose{% endraw %}

{% raw %}of peace in the latter part of this reign contributed materially to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the external prosperity of Berlin. Commerce and industry, the latter{% endraw %}
{% raw %}stimulated by the exertions of Beuth, were greatly benefited by the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}construction of highroads, the foundation of the Zollverein, and the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}abolition of the monopoly of the guilds; and the city now began to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lose the official and garrison-like air with which it had hitherto{% endraw %}
{% raw %}been pervaded. In 1838 the railway to Potsdam was opened. From{% endraw %}
{% raw %}201,000 in 1819 the population had in 1840 increased to 329,000.{% endraw %}
