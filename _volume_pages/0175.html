{% raw %}Konigs-Platz. BERLIN. Section 6. 137{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a lantern encircled with columns, which is in turn surmounted by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}an imperial crown. At the corners are four boldly designed towers,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}195 ft. high.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The chief (W.) Fac/ade, turned towards the Konigs-Platz, with a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portico borne by six columns, is the richest in plastic adornment.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}To the right and left of the door are reliefs, by 0. Lessing, of the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rhine and the Vistula, leaning respectively against an oak-tree and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a pine-tree, in the branches of which hang the arms of the German{% endraw %}
{% raw %}states; above the door is a figure of St. George (with the features{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of Bismarck), bearing the imperial banner, designed by Siem.ering;{% endraw %}
{% raw %}in the pediment is a relief by Schaper, representing Art and In¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}dustry protected by Germanic warriors; on the apex of the pedi¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ment is a colossal Germania on horseback, bearing shield and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}banner and escorted by two genii, by R. Begas (in copper).— Over{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the S. door is a lion guarding the regalia, by Klein; over the N.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}door a figure of Truth, by Briitt. — On the E. facade is a portico,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}beneath which is a covered carriage-way. At the sides are huge{% endraw %}
{% raw %}representations of the imperial coat-of-arms protected by two{% endraw %}
{% raw %}knights, and at the foot of the approach on each side are bronze{% endraw %}
{% raw %}candelabra, surmounted by figures of Victory dispensing laurels,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}by A. Vogel. Above are two mounted heralds (in copper), by Maison.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The exterior of the corner-towers also deserves notice. Above{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the architrave supported by columns rising from the basement story{% endraw %}
{% raw %}are 16 figures typifying the different industries and occupations of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the German people, by Behrens, Diez, Eberle, Eberlein, Lessing,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Maison, Schlierholz, and Volz. Between these are the names of{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the German princes reigning in 1871. — The windows of the prin¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}cipal floor show the arms of the federal states and free cities.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The Interior (adm., see p. 39), the decoration of which is still{% endraw %}
{% raw %}incomplete, is entered by Portal V, on the N. The N. Vestibule is{% endraw %}
{% raw %}adjoined to the right by a Waiting Room, whence we ascend to the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}principal floor. Here w'e enter the *Wandel-Halle, or Promenade{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hall, which rises through two stories and runs N. and S. for the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}length of 315 ft. The floor is inlaid with coloured marble. At{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the sides, above, are galleries. The central portion consists of an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}octagon (82 ft. high, and 75 ft. in diameter), surmounted by a dome{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and separated from the side-halls by galleries and rows of columns.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In the centre is a marble monument to Emp. William I. by Pfuhl{% endraw %}
{% raw %}(1905). Above hangs a huge bronze chandelier (25 ft. in diameter){% endraw %}
{% raw %}by Riedinger of Augsburg. The decorative figures above the four{% endraw %}
{% raw %}angle-recesses of the dome are by O. Lessing. The doors on the E.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}of the octagon lead into the Hall of the Diet (see p. 138), while to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the W. is the main entrance (from the Konigs-Platz), which is used{% endraw %}
{% raw %}on ceremonial occasions only. The general effect of this hall will{% endraw %}
{% raw %}he greatly enhanced when its colour-decoration is completed by{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ceiling-paintings and stained-glass windows.{% endraw %}
